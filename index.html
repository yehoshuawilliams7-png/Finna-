How this manual works

Security is networking, systems administration, and programming, all aimed at one question: how does this break, and how would I know? Each module below has a goal, what to study, what to build, and what tools to get hands-on with. Below the roadmap you'll find the full knowledge map — every concept, protocol, and tool worth knowing the name of, grouped by domain — plus practice ranges, a certification ladder, and a reading list. roadmap

Five stages, in order

Don't skip to offense/defense content before stage two is solid — that's the most common dead end. It produces people who can run a tool but can't explain what it did.

STAGE 01 — FOUNDATION
Computers, before security
8–10 WEEKS
~10 HRS/WK
+

Goal: Comfortable in a Linux terminal, understand how a request travels across a network, and can read/write basic scripts. Nobody reasons about an attack on a system they don't understand.

Study

  • Linux fundamentals — filesystem, permissions, processes, package managers
  • Networking core — OSI model, TCP/IP, subnetting, DNS, HTTP, routing
  • Hardware & OS basics — components, OS-independent troubleshooting
  • Scripting — Bash and Python, enough to automate small tasks

Build

  • Daily-drive Linux for one month, not a VM you avoid
  • Map your home network: router, devices, IPs, subnets by hand
  • Script that scans a directory and reports file types/sizes
PROJECT: OverTheWire Bandit, all 34 levels
STAGE 02 — CORE SECURITY
How systems actually fail
10–12 WEEKS
~10 HRS/WK
+

Goal: Understand the CIA triad, common vulnerability classes, and core cryptography well enough to explain them — not just name them.

Study

  • OWASP Top 10 — injection, broken auth, XSS, SSRF, misconfig
  • Cryptography — symmetric/asymmetric, hashing, PKI, TLS handshake
  • Auth & access — Kerberos, RADIUS, LDAP, SSO, MFA, least privilege
  • Threat modeling — CIA triad, defense in depth, zero trust basics

Build

  • Stand up a vulnerable web app locally, exploit each OWASP category
  • One-page threat model for an app you use daily
  • Home lab: router + 2 VMs (attacker, target) via VirtualBox/Proxmox
PROJECT: full exploit write-up of OWASP Juice Shop
STAGE 03 — OPERATIONAL
Pick a lane, go hands-on
12–16 WEEKS
~12 HRS/WK
+

Goal: Real, demonstrable skill in one specialization — offense, defense, or GRC (see paths below). Depth is what gets interviews, not shallow familiarity with all three.

Study

  • Offense: recon, enumeration, privilege escalation, web attacks
  • Defense: SIEM/SOAR, detection engineering, threat hunting, DFIR
  • GRC: NIST CSF, ISO 27001, risk frameworks, audit basics
  • Wireless & network attacks — relevant across all three lanes

Build

  • 25+ machines on a practice range in your chosen lane
  • Public write-up blog or GitHub for every box/lab solved
  • Join a CTF team, play 3–4 events
PROJECT: home SOC — Wazuh + ELK ingesting lab logs
STAGE 04 — PROFESSIONAL DEPTH
Build a portfolio, get certified
12+ WEEKS
~10 HRS/WK
+

Goal: Proof, not just claims. A certification plus a portfolio of real write-ups separates an applicant from a hire.

Study

  • Target one certification matching your path (ladder below)
  • Cloud security basics — shared responsibility, IaC, serverless risk
  • Incident report writing — half the job in every security role

Build

  • Portfolio site: 5–8 detailed write-ups, not 30 shallow ones
  • One real fix or finding contributed to an open-source project
  • Present a lab finding or CTF solve at a local meetup
PROJECT: pass one entry-to-mid certification exam, end to end
STAGE 05 — INTO THE FIELD
Get hired, then keep learning
ONGOING
+

Goal: Land an entry role and treat the first 18 months as the real continuation of this manual. Nothing here replaces production experience.

Target roles

  • SOC Analyst (Tier 1) — most common entry point
  • Junior Penetration Tester / Security Consultant
  • IT role with security responsibilities (sysadmin → security)
  • GRC Analyst / Compliance Associate

Keep building

  • Follow CVE disclosures in your stack weekly
  • Pursue the next certification rung once on the job
  • Mentor someone earlier in the manual — teaching exposes gaps

Pick your lane

"Cybersecurity" is too broad to specialize in directly. Pick the lane that matches how you like to think, not the one that sounds most exciting.

OFFENSE

Red Team / Pentest

You enjoy breaking things on purpose, writing exploits, thinking like an attacker. Heavy on persistence and creative problem solving.

PentesterRed TeamerAppSec
DEFENSE

Blue Team / SOC

You like patterns, logs, catching what everyone else missed. Heavy on monitoring tools, detection logic, calm under pressure.

SOC AnalystDFIRDetection Eng.
GOVERNANCE

GRC / Risk

You think in policy, process, and risk tradeoffs rather than code. Heavy on frameworks, audits, translating risk for leadership.

GRC AnalystAuditorRisk Mgmt

The certification ladder

Certifications don't replace skill, but they get a résumé past the filter. Climb in order — don't jump to advanced certs before the foundation ones, the material won't stick.

ENTRY
CompTIA Security+

Broad foundational coverage across every domain. The standard first cert — often a job listing requirement even for junior roles.

EARLY
CompTIA Network+ / Linux+ / CCNA

Worth having if stage 01 felt shaky — shores up the networking and Linux fundamentals everything else depends on.

MID — OFFENSE
eJPT → OSCP → CRT / CEH

eJPT is an accessible first practical exam. OSCP is the well-respected, genuinely hard hands-on pentest exam most red team jobs reference.

MID — DEFENSE
BTL1 → GSEC → GIAC / GPEN / GWAPT

BTL1 is practical and affordable. The GIAC family is heavier and well-regarded once you're working in the field.

MID — GRC
ISO 27001 Lead Implementer → CISA

CISA (ISACA) is the standard audit/governance credential, usually pursued once you can meet its experience requirement.

SENIOR
CISSP / CISM

Broad management-level credentials requiring ~5 years experience to fully certify. A career milestone, not a starting point.

rules_of_engagement

How to actually stick with it

Build before you certify

A certification earned without lab time produces someone who can pass a test but not do the job. Labs first, exam second, always.

Write down everything you break

A public write-up — even rough — does more for your résumé than a private pile of solved boxes, and it forces real understanding.

Stay legal, always

Only test systems you own or have explicit written permission to test. Practice ranges exist precisely so this is never a gray area.

Depth over a longer tool list

Knowing why an exploit works beats knowing the names of fifty tools. Interviewers test for the former almost every time.

Find people doing this already

A local DEF CON group, a Discord, a CTF team — community accelerates learning faster than any course, and that's where job leads come from.
